✎ Practice Questions Interview
Q: You have just been given a study to support — what are your first steps and questions?
Expected: 60–90 seconds
Model answer: First, I read the protocol and SAP thoroughly — for a cardio study, I'd focus on the primary endpoint (e.g., 3-point MACE: CV death, non-fatal MI, non-fatal stroke), the treatment arms, and the adjudication process since MACE events are typically confirmed by a blinded Clinical Events Committee. Then I confirm CDISC standards versions and identify all data sources — in cardio trials there's usually EDC data, a central lab for lipid panels and cardiac biomarkers like troponin and NT-proBNP, ECG vendor data, and often Holter monitoring. I work with the biostatistician on the ADaM specs — ADTTE for time-to-first MACE is always the most complex dataset. I ask upfront: Is there an interim analysis with alpha-spending? What's the DMC review schedule? Are MACE events adjudicated, and what's the data flow from the adjudication committee? Then I set up the programming plan, assign datasets, and create a timeline tied to the database lock and submission milestones.
Q: Have you validated others' work — e.g., overseeing a CRO's work?
Model answer: Yes. On a Phase III cardiovascular outcomes trial (CVOT), I oversaw a CRO responsible for SDTM and ADaM datasets. My process: (1) Reviewed their SDTM specs before programming — caught issues like missing SUPPAE variables for adjudication outcomes and incorrect controlled terminology for cardiac events. (2) After dataset delivery, I ran Pinnacle 21 and reviewed the report — focused on errors in AE coding (MedDRA SOC "Cardiac disorders" must be complete) and DM variable compliance. (3) Did independent spot-checks — traced 10 subjects through their MACE events from CRF to AE SDTM to ADTTE to verify event dates, censoring, and time-to-event calculations. (4) Reviewed define.xml — confirmed the ADTTE computational method described the MACE composite endpoint correctly. (5) When I found the CRO had miscoded some adjudicated MI events, I documented the issue and they corrected the mapping in the next delivery.
Q: How many studies at a time have you supported? As Lead or support programmer?
Structure your answer honestly. Example: "I've supported up to 3 studies concurrently — leading one Phase III CVOT and supporting two Phase II heart failure studies. As lead on the CVOT, I owned the SDTM/ADaM specs, assigned programming work across 3 programmers, created the ADTTE dataset for MACE myself (most complex), and managed the submission timeline with the biostatistician. On the HF studies as support, I programmed ADLB (NT-proBNP, troponin T) and ADVS (blood pressure, heart rate) and produced the lab shift tables. Balancing multiple studies meant prioritizing DMC data cut deadlines and communicating dependencies early."

📊 SDTM — Core Concepts CDISC

SDTM (Study Data Tabulation Model) standardizes raw clinical data for regulatory submissions. In a cardiovascular trial, SDTM organizes everything from ECG findings (EG domain) to cardiac biomarker labs (LB) to MACE adverse events (AE) into a consistent, reviewable format.

RFXSTDTC vs RFSTDTC
RFSTDTC = Subject reference start date. Often informed consent. It's the study anchor date.
RFXSTDTC = Date of first study treatment. Always derived from EX domain — MIN(EXSTDTC) where treatment was administered.

Cardio example: In a CVOT, a patient may consent on 2024-01-15 (RFSTDTC), go through screening ECGs and lab draws, then receive their first dose of study drug on 2024-02-01 (RFXSTDTC). The 17-day gap is the screening period. Treatment-emergent AEs are anchored to RFXSTDTC, not RFSTDTC.

Annotated CRF (aCRF)

Annotate: Collected fields mapped to SDTM (e.g., "AE.AETERM", "LB.LBORRES", "VS.VSORRES"), domain names, codelist references.

Do NOT annotate: Derived variables (AGE, --DY, EPOCH, AESTDY), page headers, CRF instructions. In a cardio CRF, the troponin T result field gets annotated to LB.LBORRES, but the derived LBSTRESN (standardized result) does not.

Pinnacle 21 (P21)

Industry-standard CDISC validation tool. Checks datasets against IG rules and FDA business rules. In a cardio submission, I pay special attention to: AE domain completeness (all cardiac events coded to MedDRA), EX domain for exposure calculations (used for RFXSTDTC), and DM for death dates (critical for MACE analysis).

✎ SDTM Practice Questions Interview
Q: Have you reviewed and written SDTM specs? Give an example of a difficulty.
Model answer: Yes. A particularly challenging spec was for a cardiovascular outcomes trial where we had adjudicated MACE events from a Clinical Events Committee (CEC). The CEC data arrived in a non-standard format with separate adjudication outcome variables that needed to map to SUPPAE (supplemental AE qualifiers). The difficulty was deciding how to store the adjudication result — QNAM="ADJUDOUT", QLABEL="Adjudication Outcome", QVAL="CONFIRMED MI" — and ensuring the RELREC linked each SUPPAE record back to the correct AE record via AESEQ. Additionally, mapping partial onset dates for cardiac events was tricky — a patient might report chest pain starting "sometime in March" which needs an imputation rule documented in the reviewer's guide. The key was collaborating with the CEC data manager and biostatistician early.
Q: What are the classes of SDTM domains? Name 1–2 from each class.
Model answer: Six classes: Interventions (EX — drug exposure, CM — concomitant meds like statins and antihypertensives), Events (AE — including cardiac events like MI and stroke, DS — disposition including death), Findings (LB — labs like troponin and NT-proBNP, EG — 12-lead ECG with QTcF intervals), Special Purpose (DM — demographics, SV — subject visits), Trial Design (TS — trial summary showing indication "Acute Coronary Syndrome", TV — trial visits), Relationship (RELREC, SUPPAE — where we store adjudication outcomes). The class determines the structure — Findings always have --TESTCD/--TEST/--ORRES.
Q: What is the SDTM Implementation Guide?
Model answer: The SDTM IG provides specific instructions for implementing SDTM in human clinical trials. It defines domain structures, variable-level metadata, assumptions, business rules, and examples. Common versions: IG 3.2, 3.3, 3.4. For our cardiovascular outcomes trial, we used IG 3.3 which introduced the CE (Clinical Events) domain — useful for storing adjudicated MACE events separately from investigator-reported AEs. Always confirm the IG version with the sponsor before starting specs, as it determines which domains and variables are available.
Q: What is an expected variable in SDTM?
Model answer: An expected variable should be included in the domain if the data was collected or can be derived. If it's not applicable, it should still be included as an all-null column. If you omit it entirely, you must explain why in the Reviewer's Guide. For example, in the AE domain for a cardio trial, AESEV (severity) is expected — if your study uses CTCAE grading instead of mild/moderate/severe, AESEV may be all null, but you still include the column and explain the grading system in the reviewer's guide.
Q: Have you created or reviewed define documents? What was the process for creating the define for SDTM?
Model answer: Yes. For our CVOT submission, I created the SDTM define.xml using the Pinnacle 21 define editor. Process: (1) Populated the define from our SDTM specs — every dataset, variable, codelist, and origin type. (2) Added Value Level Metadata for findings domains like LB and VS where variable-level detail varies by test (e.g., LBORRES for Troponin T has different units/ranges than for LDL-C). (3) Wrote computational methods for derived variables — e.g., AGE derivation, study day calculations. (4) Generated the XML and validated with P21 define checks. (5) Opened in a browser to verify rendering, hyperlinks, and codelist displays. (6) Cross-checked that every dataset in the define matched our actual XPT transport files — no extras, no missing. (7) Had the biostatistician review the final define for completeness.
Q: Any experience with the Reviewer's Guide?
Model answer: Yes, I've authored both the cSDRG (clinical Study Data Reviewer's Guide for SDTM) and the aSDRG (for ADaM). The Reviewer's Guide helps FDA reviewers understand our data. For the CVOT, key sections included: explanation of the MACE adjudication data flow (CEC outcomes stored in SUPPAE), documentation of partial date imputation rules for cardiac event onsets, justification for accepted P21 warnings, non-standard variables and why they were needed, and the relationship between the CE domain (adjudicated events) and AE domain (investigator-reported). I also documented our approach to exposure calculations and how RFXSTDTC was derived for subjects who had dose interruptions.

1. In a CVOT, adjudicated MACE outcomes are typically stored in:
  • DM domain as a flag variable
  • DS domain under DSSCAT='MACE'
  • SUPPAE (as supplemental qualifiers to AE) or CE (Clinical Events) domain
  • LB domain as a test result
Adjudicated MACE outcomes go in SUPPAE (linked to the AE record via AESEQ) or in the CE (Clinical Events) domain introduced in SDTM IG 3.3. CE is specifically designed for events confirmed by a committee, separate from investigator-reported AEs.
2. Which should NOT be annotated on the aCRF?
  • AESTDY (study day of AE start — derived from dates)
  • AETERM (adverse event verbatim term)
  • EGTEST (ECG test name)
  • LBORRES (lab original result)
AESTDY is derived (calculated as AESTDTC - RFSTDTC + 1). Derived variables are not annotated on the aCRF. Only variables that directly correspond to collected CRF fields get annotations.
3. RFXSTDTC for a patient in a cardio trial is derived from:
  • The consent date from the DM CRF page
  • The earliest EXSTDTC in EX where study drug was administered
  • The randomization date from DS
  • The first vital signs measurement date
RFXSTDTC = date/time of first study treatment, always from EX. In a CVOT with a run-in period, a patient may be randomized on Day -7 but not receive active treatment until Day 1 — RFXSTDTC reflects the actual first dose.
4. In TS domain, partial dates for study start date (SSTDTC) are:
  • Acceptable if month and year are present
  • Acceptable if year is present
  • Imputed using the 15th of the month
  • Not acceptable — FDA requires complete YYYY-MM-DD
FDA requires a complete date for study start date in TS. No partial dates are acceptable. Work with data management to obtain the full date.
5. In a CVOT, a patient discontinues study drug after an MI but continues in follow-up for vital status. How is this captured in DS?
  • Single DS record: DSSCAT="STUDY", DSDECOD="ADVERSE EVENT"
  • No DS record needed until study completion
  • Two DS records: one for DSSCAT="TREATMENT" (DSDECOD="ADVERSE EVENT") and one for DSSCAT="STUDY" (DSDECOD="COMPLETED" when follow-up ends)
  • DS record only for treatment discontinuation
CVOTs typically track treatment and study disposition separately. A patient can discontinue treatment (DSSCAT="TREATMENT", DSDECOD="ADVERSE EVENT") while still completing the study follow-up period (DSSCAT="STUDY", DSDECOD="COMPLETED"). This is critical because time-to-event analyses need vital status even after treatment stops.

✎ ADaM Practice Questions Interview
Q: What is ADSL typically used for?
Model answer: ADSL is the subject-level analysis dataset — one row per subject. It's the foundation for all other ADaM datasets. In a CVOT, ADSL contains: demographics (age, sex, race, region), treatment assignments (TRT01P/TRT01A for the drug arm like "Ticagrelor 90mg" vs "Placebo"), population flags (SAFFL, ITTFL), key dates (TRTSDT, TRTEDT, DTHDT, randomization date), baseline disease characteristics (LVEF, eGFR, prior MI flag, diabetes flag, baseline LDL-C), and subgroup variables used in forest plots. Every other ADaM dataset merges from ADSL to get treatment, population flags, and key dates. It's also used directly for the demographics summary table (Table 14.1.1).
Q: What is the difference between SDTM and ADaM?
Model answer: SDTM is the standardized tabulation of collected data — minimal derivation, ISO 8601 character dates, one record per observation. ADaM is analysis-ready — adds derived variables needed to produce statistical outputs. In our CVOT: SDTM AE has the raw adverse event records with character dates; ADaM ADAE adds numeric dates (ASTDT, AENDT), treatment-emergent flag (TRTEMFL), severity grade, and treatment at time of AE. SDTM EX has raw exposure records; ADaM ADSL derives TRTSDT/TRTEDT and population flags from EX and other domains. The biggest difference is ADTTE — it doesn't exist in SDTM. ADTTE is entirely derived by combining adjudicated events from AE/CE, death dates from DS/DM, and calculating time-to-first-MACE.
Q: Give an example of a particularly complex dataset.
Model answer: ADTTE for the 3-point MACE endpoint in our cardiovascular outcomes trial was the most complex dataset I've programmed. The complexity came from multiple sources: (1) MACE is a composite — CV death, non-fatal MI, non-fatal stroke — each component comes from different source records (AE domain for MI and stroke, DS for death, plus adjudication committee data in SUPPAE). (2) We used adjudicated events, not investigator-reported, requiring a join to the CEC dataset and logic to prefer adjudicated outcomes. (3) Censoring rules were nuanced: censor at last known alive date if no event, but for subjects who withdrew consent, censor at withdrawal date. (4) Partial event dates required imputation — a reported MI onset of "2024-03" was imputed to 2024-03-01 for the primary analysis. (5) Multiple PARAMCD parameters: MACE3 (3-point), CVDEATH, NONFMI, NONFSTRK, plus sensitivity analyses with different censoring rules.
Q: What are AVAL and AVALC used for?
Model answer: AVAL is the numeric analysis value — the primary variable in BDS datasets. For ADLB in a cardio trial, AVAL holds the lab result in standard units (e.g., Troponin T in ng/L, LDL-C in mmol/L). AVALC is the character analysis value — used when the result is categorical (e.g., "POSITIVE"/"NEGATIVE" for a qualitative troponin test) or when both representations add information. At least one must be populated per record. AVALC should not simply be the character version of AVAL — that adds no analytical value. In ADEG, AVAL might be QTcF in ms, while in ADTTE, AVAL is the time-to-event in days.
Q: What treatment variables are in ADAE? E.g., in crossover trials?
Model answer: In a simple parallel-group CVOT, ADAE gets TRTA (actual treatment) and TRTP (planned treatment) from ADSL's TRT01A/TRT01P — every AE is attributed to the single treatment the subject received. In a crossover trial (e.g., testing two antiplatelet regimens), TRTA changes by period. The AE is attributed to the treatment the subject was on when the AE started. If a subject was on Drug A in period 1 (weeks 1-12) and Drug B in period 2 (weeks 13-24), an AE starting in week 10 gets TRTA="Drug A" and an AE starting in week 15 gets TRTA="Drug B". This requires period-level treatment variables (TRT01A, TRT02A) and date-based logic to determine which period the AE falls in.
Q: What derived variables are in ADLB? What analysis flags?
Model answer: Key derived variables in ADLB for a cardio trial: BASE (baseline value — last non-missing before TRTSDT), CHG (AVAL - BASE), PCHG (percent change — critical for LDL-C reduction endpoints), AVISIT/AVISITN (windowed analysis visit). Key flags: ABLFL (baseline record flag), ANL01FL (record selected for analysis within visit window), ANRIND (reference range indicator: LOW/NORMAL/HIGH for shift tables), BNRIND (baseline range indicator). For cardio-specific criteria: CRIT1="Troponin T > 99th percentile ULN", CRIT1FL=Y/N — used to identify myocardial injury. Also derive R2BASE (ratio to baseline) for biomarkers like NT-proBNP.

📊 TFLs — Concepts & Cardio Examples SAS Cardio
TFL Workflow
1. Review the shell (mock TFL) from biostat — e.g., "Table 14.3.1.1: Summary of Treatment-Emergent Adverse Events by SOC and PT — Safety Population"
2. Filter ADAE to SAFFL='Y' AND TRTEMFL='Y'. For cardio tables, also filter AEBODSYS='Cardiac disorders' for cardiac-specific tables.
3. Compute: PROC FREQ for n(%) by SOC/PT/treatment; PROC MEANS for continuous (e.g., time to onset).
4. Derive Big N from ADSL. Build display dataset with formatted "n (%)" strings.

⚙ SAS — DATA Step & PDV SAS
Data Step Execution
Compile: Scans for syntax errors, builds PDV, determines variable attributes. Compile-time statements: WHERE, FORMAT, INFORMAT, LABEL, LENGTH, RETAIN, ARRAY, DROP, KEEP.
Execute: Row-by-row through PDV. Each iteration: (1) PDV resets to missing (except RETAIN/sum vars), (2) reads record, (3) executes statements, (4) writes to output (implicit OUTPUT), (5) returns to top.
PDV, _N_, Input Buffer
PDVLogical memory area where SAS builds each observation. Contains all variables + _N_ + _ERROR_. _N_Iteration counter — auto-created by DATA step. NOT the observation number (still increments when IF filters a row out). Not written to output. Input BufferTemporary memory for raw data when reading with INFILE/INPUT. Does NOT exist when reading SAS datasets (SET/MERGE). _ERROR_Auto variable: 0 = no error this iteration, 1 = error (e.g., invalid data). Not written to output.

✎ SAS Practice Questions Interview
Q: What is the concept of the Program Data Vector (PDV)?
Model answer: The PDV is a logical memory area where SAS builds each observation one at a time during DATA step execution. It contains all variables plus automatic variables _N_ and _ERROR_. Each iteration: the PDV resets to missing (except RETAINed variables), reads a record, executes statements, and writes to output. Understanding the PDV explains why RETAIN exists — without it, the baseline troponin value I'm carrying forward would get wiped every iteration. It also explains why WHERE is faster than IF — WHERE filters before reading into the PDV, so SAS never loads non-matching rows.
Q: What is the advantage of PROC SQL for joining over DATA step MERGE?
Model answer: Three main advantages: (1) No pre-sorting — MERGE requires both datasets sorted by the BY variable, which means a separate PROC SORT step. SQL handles matching internally. (2) Multi-table joins in one step — I can join ADSL, ADAE, and ADLB in a single query. With MERGE, I'd need multiple DATA steps. (3) Many-to-many handling — DATA step MERGE produces unexpected interleaved results for many-to-many relationships; SQL correctly produces the Cartesian product. In our CVOT, I used SQL to join ADSL demographics with ADAE cardiac events and ADLB troponin results in one step to create an analysis-ready listing.
Q: What is the difference between FORMAT and INFORMAT?
Model answer: FORMAT controls how SAS displays a value — it doesn't change the stored value. INFORMAT controls how SAS reads raw data into internal representation. Example from our CVOT: When reading ISO dates from SDTM, I use INPUT(SUBSTR(aestdtc,1,10), YYMMDD10.) — the YYMMDD10. is an informat that tells SAS how to interpret "2024-03-15" as a SAS date number. Then I apply FORMAT astdt DATE9. so it displays as "15MAR2024" in output. ANYDATE informat (ANYDTDTE.) is useful when receiving external RWE data with inconsistent date formats.
Q: How would you keep only the last observation per subject?
Model answer: Two approaches: (1) DATA step: Sort by USUBJID and the ordering variable (e.g., AVISITN), then use IF last.usubjid;. (2) PROC SQL: HAVING avisitn = MAX(avisitn) with GROUP BY USUBJID. In our CVOT ADLB, I used the DATA step approach to keep the last troponin value per subject for the end-of-study analysis — sorted by USUBJID ADT, then IF last.usubjid;. The SQL approach is cleaner when you don't want to permanently re-sort the dataset.
Q: What does PROC COMPARE do and when do you use it?
Model answer: PROC COMPARE compares two SAS datasets and reports differences in structure (variables, types, lengths) and values (observation by observation). Syntax: PROC COMPARE BASE=primary COMPARE=qc LISTALL CRITERION=0.00001;. I use it for independent programming validation — the primary programmer creates ADSL, the QC programmer independently creates ADSL from the same specs, and we run PROC COMPARE. Any differences are investigated. In our CVOT, a PROC COMPARE on ADTTE revealed a 2-day discrepancy in MACE event dates for 3 subjects — turned out the QC programmer used investigator-reported dates while I correctly used adjudicated dates. Without PROC COMPARE, that error could have reached the submission.
Q: Name a procedure used to create a p-value.
Model answer: PROC FREQ with CHISQ option produces chi-square test p-values for categorical variables — I use this for comparing AE incidence rates between treatment groups. For continuous variables, PROC TTEST gives two-sample t-test p-values — used for comparing mean LDL-C change from baseline. For time-to-event endpoints in our CVOT, PROC LIFETEST produces the log-rank test p-value for the Kaplan-Meier comparison of MACE between ticagrelor and placebo. PROC PHREG gives p-values from the Cox proportional hazards model along with hazard ratios and confidence intervals.
